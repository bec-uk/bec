info_outline Welcome to the Bristol Energy Coop Monitoring System

We're developing this web interface so that anyone can take a look at how much energy our solar installations are generating! These pages are under development, and over time we aim to present the data in other interesting ways. We welcome comments about what information you would like to see and how you would like it presented.

As we add new solar installations you will see them appear on these monitoring pages, but there may be a short time lag between sites becoming operational and them appearing here.

To use these pages, press on 'Map' or 'List' on the menu above to see the sites. From there, click on the name of the site to see energy generation data for that site. A menu will appear on the left allowing you to switch between monthly headline figures and a chart.

Another interesting, but quite technical, website showing energy generation is GridWatch: http://www.gridwatch.templar.co.uk/

Here you can see measures of how much power is being generated and exported to the National Grid, broken down by different generation source. Unfortunately it doesn't show generation by solar panels at the moment.

We’re not using any data from GridWatch at the moment, but we are gathering data from:

  • Simtricity by Clean Energy Prospector - our partner in gathering data from our solar arrays
  • The forecast.io online weather service
  • The weather station atop the Create Centre roof run by Bristol City Council
  • A local weather station run by Martyn Hicks

A further in-depth resource is EnergyMap, with seven years of UK energy consumption data at a resolution of Lower Level Super Output Area (approximately 1000 people), and also aggregated FIT data, available via a Google Maps interface and also CSV download: http://energymap.theconvergingworld.org/

lightbulb_outline Some information about electricity

As you may remember from school science lessons, electricity is complicated! It’s useful to know the difference between electrical power and electrical energy when looking at some of our charts, so here’s a brief description.

Power is a measure of the amount of electricity flowing at a particular moment in time. Energy is a measure of the amount of electricity which has flowed during a period of time. If we compare with a water tap, power would be how strong the flow of water out of the tap is (related to how far you turned it on), and energy would be like the amount of water that came out of the tap in, say, 10 seconds.

Electrical power is measured in watts. The packaging for light bulbs and electric heaters will tell you how much power they use in watts, or for heaters maybe in kilowatts (1000 watts is one kilowatt). Some things use a constant amount of power, but others, like your mobile phone, will use variable amounts of power depending on what they are doing (phones use more power when the screen is on, and even more when they are doing lots of calculations as you watch a video or play a game).

Electrical energy can be measured in watt-hours or kilowatt hours (kWh). Your electricity provider probably sells you 'units' of electricity. Each 'unit' is one kWh. If you had a 2000 watt heater, and you ran it for 1 hour, you would have used 2000 watt hours or 2 kWh of energy. For a 20 watt light bulb, it would take 50 hours to use a ‘unit’ of energy; 20 watts x 50 hours = 1000 watt hours = 1 kWh.

The energy our solar panels generate varies with the amount of sunshine reaching them. On a cloudy day they will still generate some energy, but far less than on a bright, sunny day. On some of our charts we add weather icons which should help to explain why some energy or power bars are larger than others. The angle at which sunlight hits a solar panel also affects the amount of power generated - a panel facing east will generate more energy in the morning, when the sun is in the east, than it will during the afternoon when the sun is no longer shining on it directly.

Power and energy can be measured for lots of different activities; riding a bicycle, boiling an egg, driving a car, etc. As we improve this website, we hope to be able to show you how much energy our panels are generating using activities such as these which may mean more to you than kilowatt hours!
